# Methodology Draft

## Methodology

### Research Design

This study adopts a supervised machine learning research design to investigate two related but conceptually distinct prediction tasks in electric vehicle battery analytics: remaining useful life (RUL) prediction and safety risk prediction. The methodological design is motivated by the need to develop models that are not only predictively credible, but also interpretable enough to support technical reasoning in a safety-relevant context. Accordingly, the study does not treat explainability as a secondary visualization exercise. Instead, explainability is incorporated as a core analytic component for comparing predictors, interpreting model behavior, and assessing whether learned patterns are consistent with plausible battery behavior.

The study is framed around the assumption that battery monitoring data contain information relevant to both degradation progression and safety-relevant abnormality, but that these two constructs should not be conflated. In this methodology, battery health refers broadly to the inferred condition of the battery, RUL refers to the amount of service life remaining before a defined end-of-life threshold is reached, and safety risk refers to the degree of concern associated with abnormal or potentially hazardous battery behavior within an observation window. This conceptual separation is maintained throughout target definition, feature design, model training, explainability analysis, and evaluation.

Methodologically, the study follows a staged workflow. First, raw battery monitoring data are organized into a structured analytical schema. Second, target labels for RUL and safety risk are defined using explicit and reproducible rules. Third, engineered features and, where appropriate, sequence-based inputs are constructed from time-series battery data. Fourth, baseline and more advanced models are trained and compared. Fifth, explainability methods are applied to candidate models to assess global and local predictor importance. Finally, model performance is evaluated using task-specific metrics, robustness testing, uncertainty analysis, and structured error analysis. This staged design is intended to support both thesis-level transparency and later empirical implementation.

### Data Design

#### Unit of Analysis

The proposed unit of analysis is the battery observation window. An observation window is defined as a bounded segment of data for a single battery entity over a specified cycle range or time interval. Depending on dataset structure, this window may correspond to a complete charge-discharge cycle, a fixed-duration segment, or a rolling multi-cycle window. This unit of analysis is appropriate because it supports alignment between raw monitoring signals, engineered features, and prediction targets, while preserving a clear interpretation of when a prediction is made.

Where the dataset supports cycle-based monitoring, a cycle-level or short rolling multi-cycle representation is the recommended default. This choice is especially suitable for RUL analysis because end-of-life is commonly defined in relation to degradation over cycles. It also remains compatible with safety-oriented analysis because thermal and electrical abnormalities can be summarized within the same observation boundary. If a time-indexed or irregular-use dataset is ultimately selected, the observation window may instead be defined using fixed-length temporal segments, provided that the corresponding target definitions are adjusted explicitly.

#### Raw Data Structure

The expected raw data structure consists of timestamped or cycle-indexed battery monitoring records containing, at minimum, identifiers for dataset source and battery entity, together with measurements such as voltage, current, temperature, and operating state. Additional fields such as capacity, state of charge, internal resistance, ambient temperature, depth of discharge, energy throughput, and event indicators are desirable where available. The methodology assumes that the final data source may vary, and therefore the data design is expressed as a transferable schema rather than as a dataset-specific specification.

To support transparent analysis, the data architecture is separated into four analytical layers: raw measurements, windowed feature tables, target label tables, and battery metadata. This layered structure is intended to prevent confusion between directly measured variables and derived analytical constructs. It also supports reproducible joins across `dataset_id`, `battery_id`, and `window_id`, thereby enabling traceability from final model outputs back to the corresponding source data and preprocessing decisions.

#### Target Definition

Two primary dependent variables are defined for the study. The first is `target_rul`, which represents the amount of usable service life remaining from the end of the observation window until the battery reaches the defined end-of-life criterion. The preferred form of this target is a continuous regression label expressed in cycles remaining when cycle-based data are available, or in time remaining when the dataset is more naturally time-indexed. A common default end-of-life assumption is that usable capacity has fallen below 80 percent of nominal capacity, although this threshold should be replaced if the selected dataset provides a more appropriate criterion.

The second dependent variable is `target_safety_risk`, which represents the degree of safety-related concern associated with an observation window. This label is conceptually distinct from battery health and from RUL. The preferred labeling hierarchy is direct safety annotation where available, followed by event-based labels derived from known safety-relevant events, and finally proxy-based labels derived from documented thermal, electrical, or operational abnormality rules. For early-stage modeling, binary or ordinal safety labels are preferred because they are easier to audit, easier to interpret, and less likely to imply spurious precision in datasets with uncertain safety annotation. An auxiliary label, `target_eol`, may be used to support RUL derivation and internal consistency checks, but it does not replace the prognostic role of `target_rul`.

#### Preprocessing and Leakage Control

Preprocessing is designed to preserve temporal integrity and prevent leakage from future observations. The raw data are first standardized with respect to timestamp formatting, cycle indexing, missing-value codes, measurement units, and entity identifiers. The data are then ordered chronologically within each battery entity prior to segmentation into observation windows. Signal cleaning, filtering, interpolation, and resampling may be applied where required, but all such steps must be documented and justified with respect to their likely effect on battery behavior signals.

Leakage prevention is a central methodological requirement. Features are computed only from data available up to the end of the observation window. Train, validation, and test splits are defined before any global preprocessing step that could otherwise allow information from held-out data to influence training transformations. Scaling, normalization, imputation models, and feature-selection decisions are therefore fit on the training portion only and then applied to validation and test data. This is particularly important in RUL modeling, where future degradation information is needed for label construction but must never be incorporated into predictors.

### Feature Engineering

The feature-engineering strategy is designed to support both classical machine learning and advanced time-series modeling. The core principle is that features should be technically meaningful, explicitly defined, reproducible, and aligned with the observation window. The feature design is organized into four main groups: electrical behavior, thermal behavior, degradation indicators, and operational usage patterns. These groups were selected because they map onto the main pathways through which monitored battery behavior may influence the two target tasks.

Electrical behavior features summarize the battery's electrical response within an observation window, including statistics and descriptors derived from voltage and current signals. Thermal behavior features characterize temperature magnitude, variability, rise rates, excursions, and recovery patterns. Degradation indicator features capture longer-term changes in battery capability, such as capacity fade, resistance growth proxies, and efficiency-related measures. Operational usage pattern features describe how the battery is used, including charge-discharge duration, depth of discharge, rate conditions, cumulative throughput, and rest intervals. This grouping is methodologically useful because it supports later ablation analysis and enables comparison of which feature families contribute most strongly to RUL and safety risk.

The study gives particular emphasis to interpretable engineered features for two reasons. First, engineered tabular features facilitate transparent model comparison and stronger post hoc explanation analysis. Second, they support physically plausible interpretation by linking model inputs to observable electrical, thermal, and operational behavior. At the same time, the feature design remains compatible with deep learning workflows. Where sequence models are used, the methodology allows either raw or lightly processed time-series inputs, or a hybrid design in which engineered features are combined with sequence representations. This dual-view strategy allows the study to compare predictive performance and interpretability without assuming that a single input representation is optimal for all models.

Feature extraction is performed in a fixed order for reproducibility. Observation windows are defined first, followed by baseline descriptive summaries, degradation-oriented indicators, safety-oriented abnormality indicators, and usage-context variables. Features are then reviewed for redundancy, interpretability, and data availability. Each feature must be documented with its definition, data source, intended task relevance, and provenance. This documentation is necessary not only for implementation, but also for thesis reporting and future replication.

### Modeling Strategy

The modeling strategy follows a staged development plan that begins with simple baselines and progresses to more complex models only after foundational benchmarks have been established. This structure reflects the research objective of balancing predictive performance with interpretability and safety relevance. It also reduces the risk that complex models are adopted prematurely without a stable reference point for comparison.

The first stage involves separate-task baseline models for RUL regression and safety risk classification. For RUL, linear regression, regularized regression, and simple tree-based regressors provide transparent performance baselines. For safety risk, logistic regression, decision trees, and other interpretable classifiers serve as initial comparators. These models are not expected to represent the best attainable predictive performance, but they provide essential benchmarks for assessing whether more complex models offer meaningful improvements.

The second stage introduces stronger classical machine learning models trained on engineered feature tables. Candidate models may include random forest, gradient boosting, XGBoost, LightGBM, support vector methods, and similar structured-data approaches. These models are especially relevant because they often provide a favorable compromise between predictive strength and explainability, particularly when paired with model-specific or model-agnostic attribution methods.

The third stage introduces advanced time-series models where data quality and volume justify their use. Candidate architectures include recurrent neural networks such as LSTM or GRU, temporal convolutional networks, and transformer-based time-series models. These models are intended to capture temporal structure that may be partially lost in summary feature tables. However, they are treated as empirical alternatives rather than as assumed improvements, because their additional predictive power must be weighed against reduced transparency and potentially less stable explanations.

The study also considers whether the two prediction tasks should be modeled separately or jointly. Separate-task modeling is treated as the primary and most defensible starting point because RUL and safety risk are distinct outcomes with different label structures and error implications. Joint-task comparison without shared parameters may then be used to compare task-specific predictor patterns, while multi-task learning may be evaluated as an extension in which shared representations are learned across tasks. The methodological position of this study is that multi-task learning should be treated as a testable design choice rather than as an automatic improvement.

Model selection is therefore based on three criteria: predictive performance, interpretability, and safety relevance. The preferred model is not necessarily the one with the single best metric value. Rather, it is the model or group of models that provides a credible balance between accurate prediction, technically interpretable behavior, and acceptable error consequences for safety-oriented analysis.

### Explainability Strategy

Explainability is integrated into the methodology to address both scientific and practical objectives. Scientifically, explanation methods are used to identify which monitored variables and engineered features are associated with each target. Practically, they are used to determine whether model outputs can be interpreted in a way that is meaningful for battery monitoring and safety-oriented decision support. In this study, useful explainability is defined as explanation that is stable, technically plausible, and able to distinguish degradation-related effects from safety-related influences where appropriate.

The explainability workflow includes global, local, and cross-task analyses. Global explanations are used to identify dominant predictors across the dataset and to compare the overall structure of importance between RUL and safety risk models. Local explanations are used to interpret specific predictions, especially cases involving low predicted RUL, elevated safety risk, or apparent disagreement between the two tasks. Cross-task explanation analysis is used to assess whether important predictors are shared or distinct across prognostic and safety-oriented models.

The specific explanation method depends on model family. Tree-based models are explained primarily using SHAP values, permutation importance, and, where appropriate, partial dependence or accumulated local effects. Neural and hybrid models may be examined using SHAP approximations, integrated gradients, gradient-based attribution, perturbation analysis, or temporally structured masking methods. For time-series models, the methodology distinguishes between channel-level importance, temporal importance, and interaction patterns across monitored variables. Attention weights, if used in sequence models, are treated cautiously and not accepted as sufficient explanations on their own.

Explainability findings are not interpreted as proof of causality. Instead, they are treated as model-based evidence about which inputs the model relied upon under the current experimental design. The validity of these findings is strengthened by comparing explanations across model families, validation splits, and selected case studies. This approach helps reduce the risk of over-interpreting unstable or purely post hoc explanations, which is especially important in a safety-critical research setting.

### Evaluation Framework

The evaluation framework is designed to assess not only predictive accuracy, but also robustness, uncertainty, error structure, and safety relevance. Because the study addresses two distinct tasks, evaluation is conducted separately for RUL and safety risk before any cross-task synthesis is attempted. This separation is necessary to avoid masking task-specific weaknesses behind aggregated summaries.

For RUL prediction, the primary evaluation focus is regression quality. Metrics such as mean absolute error, root mean squared error, and related error summaries are used to quantify predictive accuracy. Additional attention is given to late-life prediction behavior because large errors near end-of-life may have greater practical significance than equally large errors at early degradation stages. Where relevant, subgroup analyses may be used to assess performance across different operating regimes or battery conditions.

For safety risk prediction, the primary evaluation focus is classification quality under potential class imbalance and asymmetric error consequences. Recommended metrics include precision, recall, F1-score, area under the precision-recall curve, confusion-matrix-derived summaries, and class-specific reporting for elevated-risk categories. Particular attention is given to false negatives, because failing to identify a truly elevated-risk condition may be more consequential than issuing a conservative warning. False positives are also examined because excessive over-warning may reduce practical trust and distort safety interpretation.

Beyond task-specific metrics, the study includes robustness testing across operating conditions, thermal regimes, usage patterns, and degradation stages. Uncertainty analysis is also incorporated to assess confidence calibration, prediction dispersion, and instability across repeated runs or model variants. Structured error analysis is used to identify where models fail, what kinds of batteries or windows are associated with poor predictions, and whether those failures have particular safety implications. Together, these evaluation layers provide a more defensible basis for model comparison than metric ranking alone.

### Reproducibility Considerations

Reproducibility is treated as a methodological requirement rather than a procedural afterthought. The study therefore adopts a structured experiment tracking workflow in which each experiment is assigned a unique identifier and linked explicitly to its dataset version, preprocessing logic, feature set, target definitions, split strategy, model configuration, evaluation outputs, and explanation artifacts. A recommended naming format such as `exp_YYYYMMDD_task_model_shortnote` is used to maintain consistency across models, reports, and saved results.

All experiments should record the minimum set of information required for replication, including dataset source, number of observations, battery level, feature groups, label definitions, hyperparameters, random seeds, evaluation metrics, and explainability methods. These settings should be stored in structured configuration files such as YAML or JSON. Model artifacts, preprocessing objects, feature tables, explanation outputs, and result tables should be saved in predictable locations and named with the corresponding experiment identifier to prevent ambiguity or overwriting.

Reproducibility also depends on transparent documentation of feature provenance, label construction, and split logic. For this reason, the methodology requires versioned definitions for preprocessing rules, RUL labeling assumptions, safety-risk labeling rules, and feature-extraction steps. Where dataset-specific choices are made later, those choices should be recorded as explicit study assumptions. This practice supports internal consistency during implementation and also strengthens the methodological credibility of the final thesis by ensuring that reported findings can be traced to documented analytical decisions.

### Chapter Summary

In summary, the methodology for this study is designed to support rigorous and interpretable modeling of electric vehicle battery RUL and safety risk from monitored battery data. The chapter defines a structured data design, separates target concepts clearly, prioritizes interpretable yet extensible feature engineering, adopts a staged model development workflow, integrates explainability as a core analytic element, and evaluates models using a safety-aware framework. By embedding reproducibility measures throughout the workflow, the methodological design aims to provide a credible foundation for later implementation, empirical comparison, and thesis-level reporting.
