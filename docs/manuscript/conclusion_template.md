## Conclusion

This study investigates explainable artificial intelligence for safety risk prediction and remaining useful life prediction in electric vehicle batteries. The work is motivated by the need for predictive frameworks that do not treat battery prognostics, safety-oriented prediction, and interpretability as independent concerns. Instead, the study is structured around the premise that remaining useful life and safety risk are distinct but related outcomes that should be modeled explicitly and interpreted in relation to monitored battery behavior.

At a methodological level, the study develops a framework that connects battery monitoring data, feature engineering, target definition, model development, explanation analysis, and evaluation. In this framework, remaining useful life is treated as a prognostic target defined relative to an explicit end-of-life criterion, whereas safety risk is treated as a safety-oriented prediction problem grounded in direct, event-based, or carefully documented proxy labels. The study further emphasizes that explainability should function as a central analytical component for comparing the predictors of the two tasks rather than as an optional afterthought.

The expected contribution of the study is to provide a clearer distinction among battery health, safety risk, and remaining useful life; a structured basis for comparing predictive models across these tasks; and an explanation-oriented framework for interpreting model outputs in a safety-critical battery monitoring context. If the empirical results support the proposed framework, the work may contribute both to methodological rigor in battery analytics and to more interpretable decision-support approaches in electric vehicle battery monitoring.
