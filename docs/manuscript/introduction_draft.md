# Introduction Draft

## Introduction

### Background

Electric vehicles are increasingly important in contemporary transportation systems because they offer a pathway toward lower-emission mobility and reduced dependence on conventional fossil-fuel-based transport. At the center of electric vehicle operation is the lithium-ion battery system, which determines not only vehicle range and performance but also lifecycle cost, maintenance demand, and operational reliability. For this reason, the battery is not merely an energy storage component; it is a critical subsystem whose condition strongly influences the technical and economic viability of electric mobility.

The importance of battery condition assessment has generated extensive research in battery diagnostics, prognostics, and health management. Within this broader field, three concepts are especially important and must be distinguished carefully in the present study: battery health, safety risk, and remaining useful life (RUL). Battery health refers to the condition of the battery relative to its nominal or expected performance and is commonly inferred through indicators such as capacity retention, internal resistance growth, efficiency change, and signal response variation. Remaining useful life refers to the amount of service remaining before the battery reaches a defined end-of-life criterion. Safety risk refers to the likelihood or severity of unsafe, abnormal, or hazard-relevant battery behavior, such as overheating, instability, or precursor states associated with failure-relevant conditions. These concepts are related, but they are not interchangeable. A battery may be substantially degraded without being in an immediate high-risk safety state, and a battery may display elevated safety concern without being close to end-of-life under a conventional RUL definition.

Battery monitoring systems and experimental datasets now provide increasingly rich streams of electrical, thermal, and operational information, including voltage, current, temperature, cycle count, charge-discharge behavior, and related degradation variables. These data create an opportunity for machine learning to support predictive battery analysis. However, the methodological question is no longer only whether battery outcomes can be predicted. It is also whether such predictions can be made in a way that remains technically interpretable and useful in safety-relevant contexts. This concern is especially important because black-box models may provide strong predictive performance while offering limited clarity regarding which monitored variables drive the resulting decisions.

### Problem Significance

The problem addressed in this study is significant for both practical and methodological reasons. From a practical perspective, electric vehicle batteries are safety-critical assets. Their degradation affects replacement planning, maintenance scheduling, and operational stability, while safety-related abnormality may require early warning, closer inspection, or intervention. In such contexts, model outputs are more valuable when they can support technical judgment rather than merely provide numerical prediction. A model that predicts low RUL but cannot clarify whether the prediction is associated with gradual aging, thermal stress, or abnormal electrical behavior offers only limited decision support. Similarly, a model that predicts high safety risk but cannot indicate whether the warning is driven by temperature excursions, electrical instability, degradation-related stress, or usage conditions is difficult to trust, validate, or operationalize responsibly.

From a methodological perspective, the significance of the problem lies in the need to align target definition, feature engineering, predictive modeling, and explanation analysis. Battery analytics has advanced rapidly in recent years, yet many studies continue to evaluate performance primarily in terms of accuracy or error reduction, without equivalent attention to interpretability and safety relevance. In a safety-critical setting, this is insufficient. Interpretability is not simply a desirable supplement to prediction quality; it is part of what determines whether the output of a predictive model can be reviewed, justified, and used responsibly.

The present study therefore treats explainability as a central analytic requirement. It does not assume that predictive performance alone is adequate for battery monitoring research. Instead, it works from the premise that useful battery prediction should support the interpretation of monitored electrical, thermal, degradation-related, and operational variables in relation to both remaining useful life and safety risk. This is particularly important in an academic study that aims to contribute not only a model, but also a defensible methodological framework.

### Research Gap

Although the literature on battery prognostics, battery safety analysis, and explainable artificial intelligence has expanded considerably, the integration of these areas remains limited. Existing studies have contributed substantial knowledge in separate domains. Many studies have addressed battery RUL prediction, often using degradation data and machine learning techniques to forecast end-of-life progression. Other studies have focused on battery faults, anomalies, thermal runaway precursors, or abnormal-state detection as safety-related problems. A growing body of research has also examined explainable AI in prognostics and in safety-critical machine learning more broadly. However, the major gap does not lie in the absence of these research streams individually. Rather, it lies in their incomplete integration within a single research framework for electric vehicle batteries.

Several more specific gaps are relevant. First, RUL prediction and safety-oriented prediction are commonly modeled as separate tasks with different assumptions, different feature sets, and different evaluation priorities. This leaves unresolved the question of whether the same monitored battery variables can support both tasks and whether their dominant predictors are shared or task-specific. Second, safety risk is often less explicitly operationalized than RUL. Whereas RUL is usually tied to a documented end-of-life threshold, safety risk may be represented indirectly through fault conditions, event proximity, proxy rules, or abnormal operating states that are not always defined consistently. Third, explainability is frequently used to interpret one model after training rather than to compare the predictive structure of multiple battery outcomes. As a result, the literature still provides limited insight into how the predictors of RUL differ from, or overlap with, the predictors of safety risk. Fourth, high predictive performance does not automatically provide useful support for safety-relevant decision making if the model’s reasoning remains opaque or unstable.

Accordingly, the precise gap addressed by this study is the absence of a clearly structured research framework for electric vehicle batteries in which monitored battery variables are processed into explicit predictors, RUL and safety risk are modeled as separate dependent variables, the contribution of those predictors is interpreted using explainable AI, and predictive performance is evaluated together with interpretability and safety relevance. This gap is methodologically important because it affects how results are defined, compared, and interpreted. It is practically important because it affects whether predictive outputs can support technical review and safety-oriented battery monitoring.

### Study Objectives

In response to this gap, the general objective of the study is to develop and evaluate an explainable machine learning framework that predicts remaining useful life and safety risk in electric vehicle batteries from battery monitoring data and engineered features, while identifying the variables that contribute most strongly to each prediction task.

More specifically, the study pursues five objectives. First, it aims to construct a documented analytical dataset that includes cleaned battery monitoring inputs, engineered predictors, and explicit definitions of `target_rul` and `target_safety_risk`. Second, it aims to develop and evaluate predictive models for RUL using monitored electrical, thermal, degradation-related, and operational variables. Third, it aims to develop and evaluate predictive models for safety risk using battery monitoring inputs and safety-relevant engineered indicators. Fourth, it aims to apply explainable AI methods to quantify and compare the contribution of input variables and engineered features to the two prediction tasks. Fifth, it aims to compare candidate models in terms of predictive performance, interpretability, and relevance to battery degradation and safety monitoring.

These objectives also define the central logic of the study. The purpose is not merely to build accurate predictors, but to determine how model structure, target definition, and feature relevance differ across a long-horizon prognostic task and a safety-oriented task. In doing so, the study seeks to answer not only which models perform well, but also which monitored variables matter most, which explanatory patterns are stable, and whether the resulting explanations are technically plausible in a battery-monitoring context.

### Expected Contributions

The study is expected to contribute at several levels. At a conceptual level, it contributes a clearer distinction among battery health, safety risk, and remaining useful life, thereby reducing the ambiguity that often arises when these terms are used interchangeably. At a methodological level, it contributes a structured framework in which data preparation, target definition, feature engineering, model development, evaluation, and explanation analysis are aligned explicitly. This is important because interpretability is influenced not only by the model itself, but also by how signals are processed, how labels are constructed, and how explanatory outputs are evaluated.

At the modeling level, the study is expected to contribute comparative evidence on the performance of baseline and advanced machine learning models for both RUL and safety risk prediction. At the explainability level, it is expected to contribute analysis of which monitored variables and engineered features drive each task, and whether the dominant predictors are shared or distinct across outcomes. At the practical level, the study is expected to contribute a more interpretable approach to battery monitoring in which predicted low RUL and elevated safety risk can be translated into technically grounded interpretations rather than treated as isolated numerical outputs.

The broader contribution of the study therefore lies in advancing battery analytics from a primarily accuracy-centered perspective toward a framework that gives equal attention to predictive quality, interpretability, and safety relevance. In the context of electric vehicle batteries, such a shift is important because model outputs are most useful when they support cautious, technically grounded, and reviewable reasoning in addition to numerical prediction.

The remainder of the manuscript is organized as follows.
